About Ocean Eddies Web Viewer

This is an ALPHA version of the Ocean Eddies Web Viewer!

At the moment, there is currently no user-level documentation available for this web viewer.

Caveats with this alpha version of the viewer:

  • You'll need a lot of RAM to run this viewer, something like 1 GB of spare RAM.
  • You'll also need to make sure that you have a fast enough network connection to download 80 MB of data in about one minute, since the viewer needs to download all of the tracks data ahead of time.
  • The viewer is not as fast at rendering as it could be.
  • Performance on different browser/OS configurations varies more wildly than would be the case with better cross browser/platform code. A non-Windows operating system running Google Chrome provides the best performance for this alpha version.

These issues cannot be fixed until the next version.

This page will eventually contain more extensive user documentation, as is best practice for application development. Some features/behaviors of the software may be non-obvious to a new user. But according to James Faghmous, "They're all going to be oceanographers, so they'll know." You better know, then. For now, the user documentation will be excluded, but this is a strong regret for the author of the web viewer.

Background Information

What are mesoscale ocean eddies?

Mesoscale ocean eddies are swirling disturbances in the ocean's water that are on the scale of 10 kilometers to 200 kilometers in diameter. These eddies influence a three-dimensional region of the ocean that extends from the ocean's surface to a depth of 20 to 40 meters. These eddies' structure last from 14 days to 6 months before disintegrating. Mesoscale ocean eddies are classified by their rotational direction as follows:

  • Cyclonic eddies: Counterclockwise rotation in the northern hemisphere, clockwise rotation in the sothern hemisphere
  • Anticyclonic eddies: Clockwise rotation in the northern hemisphere, counterclockwise rotation in the sothern hemisphere

Why are these eddies important?

Ocean eddies influence various characteristics of the ocean, such as heat, salt, nutrients, color, kinetic energy, subsurface density surfaces, and sea surface height. Consequentially, marine ecosystems are also influenced by mesoscale ocean eddies. For example, "one study found that 7000-year-old coral reefs were asphyxiated due to massive phytoplankton blooms, which were enhanced by a large eddy [24]."

Eddies are also the dominant source of the ocean's kinetic energy, thereby influencing other phenomenon that carry large amounts of large kinetic energy. For example, "some of the recent devastating landfalling hurricanes, including Hurricane Katrina, gained intensity in the Gulf of Mexico when passing over a warm-core eddy [18]. As a result, hurricane intensity forecasts now account for eddy activity when making projections [25]."

Accurately identifying and tracking mesoscale ocean eddies is crucial for understanding the future of marine and terrestrial ecosystems.

How are they identified?

Our methods use a suite of algorithms to automatically identify mesoscale ocean eddies by their effect on the sea surface height (SSH) data obtained from satellites. Cyclonic eddies cause a depression in the sea surface height, whereas anticyclonic eddies cause an elevation in the sea surface height. Most eddies' height variations are within the range of +/- 32 centimeters from the mean sea surface height. The resolution of the SSH maps that our algorithms use is 0.25 degrees of latitude/longitude per SSH sample, so our algorithms identify eddies that are 100 kilometers in diameter or larger and have a minimum amplitude of one centimeter.

For more information on the identification algorithms, see the publications.
